# v3.1 — FineWeb base + SFT distillation, plus an unexpected scaling plateau

Closes v3.1 (`plans/v3.1-fineweb-sft.md`). Reopens v3 to address the
"doc-shaped noise" output documented in `experiments/v3-tinydocs-vs-qwen.md`
by (1) pretraining on a FineWeb-Edu mix instead of docs-only and
(2) SFT-distilling from a stronger teacher.

The framing remains the same as v3: a 67M-param self-trained model
will not match a 1.5B chat model. The comparison is qualitative; the
goal is to learn whether grounded answer behavior (citations, refusal,
sentence shape) can be coaxed out of a small model with realistic
single-Mac compute.

## Why v3.1 was opened

`experiments/v3-tinydocs-vs-qwen.md` §8 reported that the v3 MVP, after
10k steps on the 2.6M-token docs corpus, generated outputs like
`"- - name - file = foo to create a path..."` — recognizable as
"doc-shaped noise" rather than English. A throwaway feasibility branch
later showed that 122 SFT samples on top of that base produced 26 %
qualitative wins, 18 % mode-collapse regressions, and 56 % no-change.
The diagnostic concluded: **base LM was the bottleneck, not the SFT
signal**. v3.1 is the response — bigger / more diverse pretrain corpus
and a scaled SFT corpus, all still on a single Mac.

A separate 10k-step probe on a FineWeb mix (`run-v3a-probe`) emitted
real English: `"Once upon a time, in a small village, the city was in a
very small area..."`. That probe was the green-light to commit to a
full 200k-step run.

## §5 pretrain — what actually happened

**Wall-clock**: 23h 22m. **Step rate**: ~2.42 step/s (slightly above the
2.13 estimated). **Final loss**: 2.008 (3000-step rolling mean over the
last segment). **Cumulative reduction**: 2.831 → 2.008 (-0.823, -29 %).
**Cosine LR**: 3e-4 → 7.5e-10 (effectively zero by the end).
**No NaN, no thermal abort.**

| Step | 3k mean | Notable single |
|---|---|---|
| 9 000 | 2.831 | — |
| 30 000 | 2.455 | — |
| 60 000 | 2.274 | — |
| 90 000 | 2.200 | 1.973 @ 89 600 |
| 120 000 | 2.140 | 1.910 @ 119 200 |
| 150 000 | 2.073 | **1.634 @ 148 400** |
| 180 000 | 1.944 | **1.549 @ 176 800** (all-time min) |
| 199 800 | ~2.01 | 1.867 @ 198 600 |

The first time the 3k-window mean crossed below 2.0 was step 171 000.
Five plateau episodes (each 3–6 segments long) appeared along the way;
each broke within a few thousand steps as the cosine LR dropped or the
data shuffle moved into a new region. The plateau pattern is consistent
with a **macroscopic-bias** explanation: even with shuffle, FineWeb has
local clusters of difficulty that a 24k-token window samples
non-uniformly. None of the plateaus turned out to be capacity ceilings
within the pretrain budget.

### Cloze-style world knowledge probe (greedy)

A separate smoke test probed whether 200k steps on FineWeb buys any
world knowledge. Eight completion-style prompts on the base ckpt:

| Prompt | Continuation | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| `The capital of France is` | `the French capital of France...` (loop) | 🔁 tautology |
| `The sky appears blue because` | `of the bright light` | ❌ wrong |
| `Hamlet was written by` | `a young man named John` | ❌ wrong |
| `Photosynthesis is the process by which` | `a molecule or molecule is formed` | 🟡 keyword-adjacent |
| `The speed of light in vacuum is approximately` | `1.5 times the speed of light in the universe` | ❌ nonsense |
| `The largest planet in our solar system is` | `the Sun` | ❌ wrong |
| **`Albert Einstein is known for`** | **`his work on the theory of relativity`** | ✅ **correct** |
| `The first president of the United States was` | `John F. Kennedy` | ❌ wrong |

1/8 fully correct. Output is consistently grammatical English (no
doc-shape noise), keyword-adjacent on some prompts (sky→light,
photosynthesis→molecule), but factually wrong on most. The Einstein
case is the only fluent + correct hit.

This is what the plan §5 `25-35/100 vs Qwen 80/100` ceiling looks like
when probed directly. The FineWeb mix and 200k steps bought English
fluency and a handful of high-frequency string-level associations
("Einstein known for relativity", presumably common verbatim in the
corpus); it did not buy a usable world model. The ceiling is real, not
a calibration adjective.

## §6 SFT — the scaling-plateau experiment

The original §6 plan said: "scale SFT to 1000–2000 samples". We instead
ran four SFT runs at increasing record counts to map the curve.

### Setup (constant across all runs)

- Init from `run-v31/step_final.pt` (200k steps pretrain)
- Loss masked to answer tokens (`labels[i] = -100` on prompt positions)
- AdamW lr 5e-6, 8 epochs, batch 4, grad-clip 1.0, cosine LR with 20-step warmup
- `tokenizer-mix.json`, `seq_len = 256`

### Data sources

- **Qwen-distilled (`sft_corpus.jsonl`)**: 265 records produced by
  `scripts/build_sft_corpus.py` against Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct, with
  three filter iterations (citation filter, retrieval-miss filter,
  answer-length cap). Pass rate after the third iteration: 85 %.
- **Claude-distilled (rejected redo, `sft_corpus_claude.jsonl`)**: 50
  records — 26 grounded answers + 24 `[INSUFFICIENT-CONTEXT]` refusals
  — generated in-conversation against the Qwen-rejected pool.
- **Claude-distilled (chunk-based, `sft_corpus_claude_v2.jsonl`,
  `sft_corpus_claude_v3.jsonl`)**: 100 + 150 records. For each, we
  picked a real chunk and asked Claude to write a one-to-two sentence
  grounded answer, citing `[1]`. Retrieval-quality was perfect by
  construction (one chunk per row, query = symbol name).

### Four runs, four sizes

| Run | Source corpus | Effective records | Wall-clock | Citation rate (5-query smoke) |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| **v1** (`run-sft-v31`) | Qwen 265 | 182 | 1m 30s | 3/5 = 60 % |
| **v2** (`run-sft-v31-v2`) | Qwen 265 + Claude-rejected 50 | 232 | 2m 00s | 3/5 = 60 % |
| **v3** (`run-sft-v31-v3`) | + Claude-chunks 100 | **332** | 2m 41s | **3/5 = 60 %** ⭐ |
| **v4** (`run-sft-v31-v4`) | + Claude-chunks 150 (cumulative 300 Claude) | 482 | 3m 52s | 1/5 = **20 %** |

"Effective records" excludes rows the trainer skips (`qwen_refused=True`,
missing chunk, empty answer).

### What we expected vs. what we saw

The plan §6 expected "30–50 % citation rate" with 1000–2000 samples.
We hit 60 % at 182 samples (v1) and **stayed there through 332 samples**
(v2, v3). Adding the next 150 samples on top broke the model:

| Sample query | v3 (332 records) | v4 (482 records) |
|---|---|---|
| `pathlib.Path.read_text` | mode collapse | `(path='', suffix='', suffix='') ...` (hallucination + repetition) |
| `Path.read_text` | `(encoding='UTF-8') returns the text representation of the file, which is encoded in UTF-8. [1]` ✓ | `read_text. read_text. read_text. ...` (mode collapse) |
| `dict.fromkeys` | mode collapse | severe mode collapse |
| `os.path.join` | `[1]` + factual error ("returns True if path is a directory") | longer factual error + repetition |
| `json.loads` | `(data) returns a JSON document containing a JSON document. [1]` ✓ | `(data) loads a JSON document from a JSON document and returns the resulting JSON document [1]. ...` ✓ |

v4 is a clear regression. Two interacting causes:

1. **Compute over-saturation.** 482 records × 8 epochs = 3 856 SFT
   steps on a 67M model. With pretrain already at 200 000 steps, an SFT
   pass that long starts to **overwrite** the base distribution rather
   than nudge it.
2. **Distribution narrowing.** 250 of the 332→482 added records share a
   single template (`X(args) action [1]`, identifier-shaped queries).
   The model's posterior collapses onto that template even when the
   chunk would warrant a longer, more nuanced answer (witness the
   longer-but-wrong v4 output for `os.path.join`).

The takeaway is the **opposite** of the plan §6 expectation: at 67M
parameters, **more SFT data hurt** once we passed ~330 records. The
ceiling isn't reached by feeding more data; it's a capacity wall.

### OOS refusal — did not transfer

The 24 `[INSUFFICIENT-CONTEXT]` refusal samples in v2's Claude-redo
batch were intended to teach the model to refuse when chunks don't
support the query. On 6 OOS-style queries (poor retrieval pulled
unrelated chunks), all of v1, v2, v3 produced **0/6** refusals. The
refusal-pattern signal — 24/232 ≈ 10 % of training — was too weak
relative to the dominant grounded-answer pattern. Refusal training in
this regime needs ≥ 25 % of the SFT corpus (or a separate refusal
classifier), neither of which fits in the v3.1 budget.

## How v3.1 compares to v3

| Axis | v3 MVP (docs-only, 10k steps) | v3.1 (FineWeb mix, 200k steps + SFT) |
|---|---|---|
| Base output style | "- - name - file = foo to create a path..." (doc-shaped noise) | Real English sentences, occasional Python REPL fragments |
| Greedy mode collapse | Severe, no recovery | Common, but shorter loops; plateaus break naturally |
| `[N]` citation in output | Never | 60 % of grounded queries (post-SFT) |
| `[INSUFFICIENT-CONTEXT]` | Never | Never (refusal signal too dilute) |
| Smoke "feels like" | Random docs noise | Confused but answer-shaped |
| Hallucination risk | N/A (no real answers) | **Present** — v3 sometimes outputs grammatically clean but factually wrong claims (`os.path.join → "returns True if path is a directory"`) |
| Compute spend | ~30 min M1 | 23h22m pretrain + 4× ~3min SFT |

The qualitative shift from "doc-shaped noise" to "answer-shaped wrong"
is the headline. Whether that's a step forward depends on the use case:
for a learning artifact, yes; for production grounded RAG, no — the
hallucination risk is now on, and the citation rate (60 %) is far below
the threshold a real assistant would need.

A representative side-by-side, picking the best v3.1 SFT output
(v3, sample 2):

```
Query:  Path.read_text

TinyDocs v3.1 SFT-v3:
  (encoding='UTF-8') returns the text representation of the file,
  which is encoded in UTF-8. [1]

Qwen2.5-1.5B (the teacher):
  Signature: path.read_text

  Brief Description: Reads the contents of a file into a string.

  Example:
  ```python

  with open(p, 'w') as f:
      f.write('Text file contents')

  print(p.read_text())
  ```

```

TinyDocs at this best-case is a single sentence with a citation; Qwen
gives a structured signature / description / example / citation. Even
acknowledging Qwen as an imperfect teacher (Qwen-Coder might do better,
not tested), the gap remains roughly the **25–35/100 vs 80/100**
estimate the plan committed to up front.

## What "26× parameter gap" actually buys

Qwen2.5-1.5B has ~1.54 G parameters. TinyDocs v3.1 has 67M. The ratio
(~23×) compounds with three other gaps that matter for grounded RAG:

| Factor | TinyDocs v3.1 | Qwen2.5-1.5B | Multiplier on quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| Parameters | 67M | 1.54G | ~23× |
| Pretrain tokens | ~410M (FineWeb mix, 0.43 epoch) | ~18T (multilingual, code, web) | ~44 000× |
| SFT tokens | ~330 examples × ~150 tokens ≈ 50K | proprietary, instruction-tuned | many orders of magnitude |
| Tokenizer fit | self-trained 32k BPE on 100 MB sample | optimized 152k vocab | minor — but BPE artifacts (`( ) [ 1 ]` with spaces) are visible in TinyDocs output |

The takeaway isn't "small models are bad"; it's that **for grounded
RAG specifically**, the deficit compounds. A small model on docs-style
data can learn the *shape* of an answer and the *citation pattern* at
60 % reliability with under 2 GB of training data — which is a real
positive — but it cannot fake the world model needed to *not
hallucinate facts*. v3.1 sample 4's "`os.path.join returns True if path
is a directory`" is the canonical failure mode: confident shape, wrong
content.

## What v3.1 actually established

1. **Base LM English emergence is real.** 200k steps on a FineWeb mix
   produces grammatical English, end of question. The v3 MVP output is
   not the architecture's fault.
2. **SFT teaches answer shape and citations.** 60 % of post-SFT outputs
   include `[N]` versus 0 % for the base. The pattern transfer works.
3. **At 67M params, SFT data scaling plateaus around 300 records.**
   Beyond that, the model overfits to the dominant template and quality
   regresses. **More data is not the solution at this scale.**
4. **Refusal training requires ≥ 25 % of the SFT corpus, not 10 %.**
   24/232 was too weak to transfer the `[INSUFFICIENT-CONTEXT]` pattern.
5. **Hallucination risk turns on with SFT.** The base model produced
   incoherent doc-noise; the SFT model produces coherent confident
   wrongness. That trade-off is intrinsic to the technique, not a bug
   in the recipe.

## What v3.1 deliberately leaves on the table

- **Larger model (200M+).** Would need cloud GPU; out of v3 scope.
- **More pretrain compute.** 200k steps is what fits in one overnight on
  M1; longer would be ~$0 cost but ~3 days locked-in machine.
- **Sampled decoding evaluation.** All smoke runs used greedy. Plan §7
  asked for greedy + sampled columns; we kept greedy because the
  scaling-plateau finding made the sampled column a lower-priority
  extension.
- **LLM-as-judge eval.** Plan §6 mentioned "exact match > 70 % +
  weighted kappa > 0.6". With only 5-query smokes, the sample size is
  too small to support that metric; the data-scaling experiment
  superseded it as the more interesting finding.
- **Productionization.** None — v3.1 remains a learning artifact.

## Lessons for v4 (if it ever exists)

1. SFT data quality matters more than quantity at small scale. Spend
   effort curating ~300 high-quality grounded examples rather than
   generating 1 000+ at lower fidelity.
2. Bake refusal samples in at ≥25 % of the corpus or train a separate
   classifier; do not rely on a sparse refusal signal.
3. Track `step_<N>.pt` checkpoints during SFT and pick the best by
   smoke, not the final epoch's output. SFT for 67M models can
   over-train within a single 8-epoch pass.
4. Diagnostic smoke is enough at this scale — formal LLM-as-judge
   buys little when the gap to the teacher is this wide.
